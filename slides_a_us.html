Nonviolent protest and local policy response in the United States, 2017–2023

The Question

Does nonviolence move local policy more than violence does?

Two literatures pull in opposite directions.

Premium

Nonviolent discipline broadens the coalition that moves elite decision-makers (Chenoweth & Stephan; Wasow; Gillion).

Backlash

Violent protest triggers fear-driven policy spending and electoral retrenchment (Enos, Kaufman & Sands; Collins & Margo).

Three Hypotheses

Named, not numbered.

Nonviolence Premium

Conditional on intensity, nonviolent protest produces a larger local-spending response than violent protest.

Intensity Equivalence

Conditional on intensity, the arm contrast is zero.

Violence Backlash

Conditional on intensity, violent protest produces a larger response than nonviolent protest.

Design

The estimand is the arm contrast, not either ATT alone

Δ = ATTnv − ATTv on Census-of-Governments per-capita public-safety spending.

Treatment per arm: first year a county crosses the 90th percentile of nonviolent (or violent) per-capita intensity. Callaway–Sant'Anna ATT(g,t), aggregated to a simple ATT, clustered by county.

Reading the Result

Most consistent with Violence Backlash, but unable to reject Intensity Equivalence

Caveat. The violent-arm point estimate of $12,797 should be read as an upper bound on what violence could plausibly move in the absence of confounders.

Two-door story: nonviolent protest enters the budget cycle gradually through electoral anticipation; violent protest enters sharply through public-safety salience. A seven-year panel dominated by 2020 cannot separate them.

Bottom Line · Two-Paper Headline

Once nonviolence is treated as a within-unit treatment effect rather than a campaign-type description, the Nonviolence Premium does not survive at either scale.

The cross-national companion paper (A_MM) returns the same null on V-Dem polyarchy across four estimators.
