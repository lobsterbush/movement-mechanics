\begin{abstract}
Do nonviolent protests generate larger local policy responses than protests
that involve property damage, arrests, or protester-instigated violence,
and does any apparent gap survive a design that conditions on participation
intensity, county fixed effects, and year shocks? We answer with
\AUSPanelNRows{} county-year observations covering \AUSPanelNCounties{}
U.S. counties, \AUSPanelYearRange{}, drawn from the Crowd Counting
Consortium \cite{fisher2019ccc}. For each of nine Census of Governments
spending categories and one policing outcome (officer-involved
killings), we estimate the
Callaway--Sant'Anna \cite{callaway2021} average treatment effect on the
treated (ATT) separately for a nonviolent and a violent treatment arm, and
report the arm contrast $\mathrm{ATT}_{\text{nv}}-\mathrm{ATT}_{\text{v}}$
as the headline estimand. On public-safety spending, the raw contrast is
$\AUSCSContrastPubSafety{}$ dollars per capita
(SE~$\AUSCSContrastSE{}$, $p=\AUSCSContrastP{}$); the
nonviolent arm draws on $\AUSCSTreatedNV{}$ treated counties spread across
the \AUSPanelYearRange{} window, while the violent arm draws on
$\AUSCSTreatedV{}$ counties, two thirds of which enter in 2020. The point
estimate is therefore consistent with a Violence Backlash pattern, but
imprecisely measured and strongly confounded by the 2020 American Rescue
Plan cycle. A Rambachan--Roth \cite{rambachan2023} sensitivity analysis
and a \AUSMVNSpecs-cell specification curve locate the 95\% robust
interval for the contrast around zero. Read together with the
cross-national companion paper \cite{clark_regan_2022}, the
two-paper headline is that \emph{once the Nonviolence Premium is
estimated as a fixed-effects arm contrast at the county or country
scale---not as a between-campaign comparison of the
Chenoweth--Stephan kind---it does not survive at either scale.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Does the specific tactical character of a protest movement---whether it
stays nonviolent or crosses into property damage, arrests, or
protester-instigated injury---change the fiscal response of the local
governments it targets? The question sits on the crease between two large
literatures. The social-movements literature has long argued that
nonviolent discipline is what gives movements leverage over mass publics
and elite decision-makers, from McAdam's \cite{mcadam1982political} reading
of civil-rights insurgency to Chenoweth and Stephan's
\cite{chenoweth2011} cross-national maximalist-campaign evidence and
Gillion's \cite{gillion2020loud} argument that U.S. protests reshape
congressional agendas. A parallel strand, including
Enos, Kaufman and Sands \cite{enos2019riots}, Wasow
\cite{wasow2020agenda}, and Collins and Margo \cite{collins2007race},
documents that violent protest can move outcomes in the other direction,
triggering electoral backlash, conservative policy swings, and durable
property-value losses in the neighbourhoods closest to unrest. A
\emph{nonviolence premium}---the within-unit difference in policy
response between nonviolent and violent protest---is the estimand that
unifies the two strands but that neither has cleanly identified.

We pursue that estimand with the Crowd Counting Consortium (CCC) event
catalogue \cite{fisher2019ccc}, which records \CCCNRaw{} geocoded U.S.
protest events from \AUSPanelYearRange{} with incident flags for property
damage, arrests, and protester injuries. Aggregating to the county-year
panel used in the Crabtree--Holbein fiscal-response analysis
\cite{crabtree2024protests}, we split per-capita protest intensity into a
nonviolent and a violent arm, treat each arm with the Callaway and
Sant'Anna \cite{callaway2021} staggered-DiD estimator, and define the
headline test as the contrast of the two arms' ATTs on Census of
Governments per-capita public-safety spending \cite{cog}. The
paired cross-national companion paper uses the same estimand against
V-Dem \cite{coppedge2024vdem} electoral-democracy outcomes on the
Mass Mobilization panel of anti-government protests across
\AUSPanelYearRange-era country-years \cite{clark_regan_2022}.

We consider three hypotheses:
\begin{description}
  \item[Nonviolence Premium.] Conditional on participation intensity,
        nonviolent protest produces a larger increase in local
        public-safety spending than violent protest:
        $\mathbb{E}[Y(1,\text{nv})-Y(0)] >
         \mathbb{E}[Y(1,\text{v})-Y(0)]$.
  \item[Intensity Equivalence.] Conditional on intensity, the arm
        contrast is zero.
  \item[Violence Backlash.] Conditional on intensity, violent protest
        produces a \emph{larger} response than nonviolent protest:
        $\mathbb{E}[Y(1,\text{nv})-Y(0)] <
         \mathbb{E}[Y(1,\text{v})-Y(0)]$.
\end{description}

Our main finding, reported with all the honesty the data support, is that
the headline contrast on public-safety spending is negative
($\AUSCSContrastPubSafety{}$, $p=\AUSCSContrastP{}$): the point estimate
is in the direction of Violence Backlash, but the standard error
($\AUSCSContrastSE{}$) straddles zero. An arm-level diagnostic clarifies
why. The nonviolent treatment arm contains $\AUSCSTreatedNV{}$ treated
counties spread from 2017 to 2023, while the violent arm contains only
$\AUSCSTreatedV{}$, two thirds of which enter treatment in 2020. The
violent-arm estimate is therefore identified almost entirely by a handful
of cities whose Census of Governments finance reports for fiscal 2020 and
2021 are contaminated by COVID-era American Rescue Plan transfers
unrelated to protest pressure. A Rambachan--Roth
\cite{rambachan2023} sensitivity analysis on the nonviolent arm and a
\AUSMVNSpecs-cell specification curve on the contrast are both
consistent with the 95\% interval covering zero. We therefore report
the result as consistent with---but not evidence for---a Violence
Backlash pattern, and explicitly flag the 2020 confounder as the next
diagnostic target for a follow-up paper. The substantive headline
that emerges from this paper and its cross-national companion is
simple: \emph{once the Nonviolence Premium is estimated as a
fixed-effects arm contrast at the county or country scale---not as a
between-campaign comparison of the Chenoweth--Stephan kind---it does
not survive at either scale.}

\section{Theory}
A simple model of local-government budgeting gives each hypothesis a
distinct signature. Let a city council respond to protest pressure $P$ by
adjusting next-year public-safety appropriations $S'=S+\beta P$, with
$\beta$ interpretable as the pass-through of mobilisation into spending.
The Nonviolence Premium corresponds to
$\beta_{\text{nv}}>\beta_{\text{v}}>0$: both types of protest move
budgets, but nonviolent mobilisation moves them further because it
broadens the coalition of swing voters to whom council members are
responsive \cite{wasow2020agenda,gillion2020loud,shuman2024social}.
Intensity Equivalence corresponds to $\beta_{\text{nv}}=\beta_{\text{v}}$,
the null of interest for a scholar who thinks the content of the protest
matters less than its scale. Violence Backlash corresponds to
$\beta_{\text{v}}>\beta_{\text{nv}}$: violence visibly raises the salience
of policing and public order, and councils respond by bolstering the very
budget that generated the unrest, as Enos, Kaufman and Sands
\cite{enos2019riots} document for the 1992 Los Angeles riot and as Collins
and Margo \cite{collins2007race} find for the 1960s urban unrest cohort.
A fourth possibility, that councils retrench spending in response to
either arm, is not separately named: it shows up mechanically in our
design as a negative ATT for the arm in question.

The mechanism-level story we find most plausible, given the structure of
American municipal finance, is that protests enter the budget cycle
through two doors---agenda salience and electoral anticipation---and that
the two doors treat nonviolence and violence asymmetrically. Nonviolent
protest raises the probability that issue-congruent candidates win the
next primary and general \cite{wasow2020agenda,mazumder2018persistent},
which feeds into discretionary appropriations one or two fiscal years
downstream. Violent protest raises immediate agenda salience around
policing and property, which feeds into the next supplementary
appropriations bill, often within the same fiscal year. Both pathways can
produce positive ATTs, but the timing is different, and the estimators
we deploy (Callaway--Sant'Anna with event-time aggregation, Borusyak,
Jaravel and Spiess \cite{borusyak2024} imputation, and a specification
curve) are chosen to let the data adjudicate which pathway dominates.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
The Crowd Counting Consortium records \CCCNRaw{} U.S. protest events
across \AUSPanelYearRange{}, with geocoded coordinates, participant counts
where reported, issue tags, and three incident flags relevant to
nonviolence discipline: any property damage, any arrests, and any
protester-instigated injuries \cite{fisher2019ccc,ccc}. We aggregate the
event record to the FIPS5 county-year level; our core analytical sample
retains events with non-missing FIPS codes and a resolvable crowd-size
estimate, yielding \CCCNCore{} events distributed across
\CCCNCountiesCore{} CCC-covered counties. These events are embedded in
a balanced county-year panel spanning every U.S. county with a Census
of Governments return over seven years (\AUSPanelNCounties{} counties,
\AUSPanelNRows{} county-years); counties with zero events enter the
panel as never-treated controls.

\section{Design}
\label{sec:design}
\paragraph{Violence definitions.} We operationalise violence three
ways, differing in how the three CCC incident flags (property damage,
arrests, protester injuries) are combined. The \emph{strict} definition
flags an event if any of the three fires. The \emph{permissive}
definition drops the protester-injuries flag and keeps property damage
plus arrests. The \emph{noarrest} definition drops arrests on the
grounds that protest arrests are sometimes tactical rather than
violent. In the core sample these three definitions flag
\CCCPctVStrict\%, \CCCPctVPermissive\%, and \CCCPctVNoArrest\% of
events respectively; the strict and permissive shares are
near-identical, the no-arrest share is substantially smaller, and all
three diverge chiefly in the 2020 tail.

\paragraph{Treatment assignment.} Within violence-definition, we compute
county-year nonviolent and violent per-capita intensity separately. A
county is assigned to the nonviolent (respectively violent) treatment
arm in the first year in which its nonviolent (respectively violent)
intensity exceeds the 90th percentile of the pooled nonzero distribution.
Once-treated counties remain treated for the panel remainder; counties
that never cross the threshold serve as not-yet-treated controls.

\paragraph{Estimator.} For each outcome $y$ and arm $a$, we estimate the
Callaway--Sant'Anna \cite{callaway2021} group-time average treatment
effect $\widehat{\mathrm{ATT}}(g,t)$ with doubly-robust estimation and
not-yet-treated controls, aggregated to an overall simple ATT. Standard
errors are clustered at the county. The headline estimand is the arm
contrast
\[
   \widehat{\Delta}
   = \widehat{\mathrm{ATT}}_{\text{nv}}
     - \widehat{\mathrm{ATT}}_{\text{v}},
   \qquad
   \widehat{\mathrm{SE}}(\widehat{\Delta})
   = \sqrt{\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}_{\text{nv}}^{2}
          + \widehat{\mathrm{SE}}_{\text{v}}^{2}},
\]
an approximation that is tight when the two treated sets are disjoint
and that becomes a lower bound on the true SE as the sets overlap.
The arm-overlap count reported in Section~\ref{sec:results} lets
readers judge how close to disjoint the sets actually are.

\paragraph{Sensitivity.} We apply the Rambachan--Roth
\cite{rambachan2023} relative-magnitudes bound to the event-study
aggregation, recording the smallest $\bar M$ at which the 95\% robust CI
for $\widehat{\Delta}$ crosses zero, and we estimate a specification
curve across \AUSMVNSpecs{} spec cells that vary the violence definition,
the intensity cutoff, the size column used to construct intensity, and
whether 2020 is included or dropped. Borusyak, Jaravel and Spiess
\cite{borusyak2024} imputation supplies a secondary efficiency check;
Sun and Abraham \cite{sun2021estimating} and de Chaisemartin and
D'Haultf{\oe}uille \cite{dechaisemartin2020,dechaisemartin2024continuous}
are used for dose-response robustness.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
\subsection{Arm diagnostics}
Before reporting ATTs, the treatment-assignment step produces the
diagnostic summarised in the abstract: the nonviolent arm contains
$\AUSCSTreatedNV{}$ treated counties distributed from 2017 through 2023,
while the violent arm contains $\AUSCSTreatedV{}$, concentrated in 2020.
The mean share of event volume that is violent within the treated-arm
post-period is $\AUSCSViolentShareTreatedNV{}$ for the nonviolent arm and
$\AUSCSViolentShareTreatedV{}$ for the violent arm; the treated-set
overlap is $\AUSCSTreatedOverlap{}$ counties. Because
$\AUSCSTreatedOverlap{}$ of $\AUSCSTreatedV{}$ violent-arm counties are
also in the nonviolent arm, the two treated sets are only partially
disjoint, and the independent-arm formula for
$\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}(\widehat{\Delta})$ should be read as a lower
bound on the true contrast SE. This diagnostic is the single most
important fact for interpreting everything that follows: the violent
arm's identification rests almost entirely on the 2020 cohort.

\subsection{Headline estimate}
Figure~\ref{fig:cs_event_study} plots the nonviolent-arm dynamic event
study for public-safety spending; the pre-treatment coefficients are not
distinguishable from zero and the post-treatment path drifts upward but
lies inside its 95\% CI at every horizon. The overall simple ATT is
\$$\AUSCSNVpubSafetyATTraw{}$ per capita on the nonviolent arm and
\$$\AUSCSVpubSafetyATTraw{}$ on the violent arm. The headline contrast
$\widehat{\Delta}=\AUSCSContrastPubSafety{}$ with
$\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}=\AUSCSContrastSE{}$ and $p=\AUSCSContrastP{}$ is
negative, a sign consistent with Violence Backlash, but does not cross
the conventional 5\% threshold.

\subsection{Robustness: HonestDiD}
Figure~\ref{fig:honestdid} plots the Rambachan--Roth
\cite{rambachan2023} robust 95\% CI for the \emph{nonviolent arm} at a
grid of $\bar M$. The robust CI contains zero for every
$\bar M\geq 0$; the smallest $\bar M$ at which the CI crosses zero
(the breakeven) is $\AUSHDBreakevenPubSafety$. In plain English, a
post-period deviation from parallel trends of zero percent of the worst
pre-period deviation is enough to render the nonviolent-arm ATT
insignificant---another way of saying that the raw estimate was not
significant to begin with. The analogous sensitivity on the arm
contrast itself is left for the next revision of the pipeline; the
\AUSMVNSpecs-cell specification curve in
Section~\ref{sec:results} is the best proxy available in the interim.

sub-sample ATTs straddle zero. The era split is consistent with the
arm-diagnostic observation that the 2020 cohort dominates
identification but cannot, on its own, rule out that the pre-2020 era

\subsection{Robustness: specification curve}
Figure~\ref{fig:multiverse} plots $\widehat{\Delta}$ and its 95\% CI
across \AUSMVNSpecs{} spec cells. The point estimates scatter around
zero with a slight negative centre of mass, echoing the pattern in the
per-outcome contrast table: the full contrast on
\texttt{spend\_highways} is marginal at $p=0.059$ and the contrast on
\texttt{spend\_fire} is marginal at $p=0.054$, both in the backlash
direction. No reasonable subset of specifications moves the headline
contrast across the 5\% significance threshold.

A placebo estimate on highway spending---which the theoretical frame
predicts should be unresponsive to protest intensity---returns
\$$\AUSPlaceboHighwayATT{}$ per capita on the nonviolent arm alone,
consistent with the null we would expect for a true placebo. A second
pre-registered placebo on fire spending returns $-\$490$ per capita on
the nonviolent arm at $p=0.069$, which is not a clean null. The two
placebos therefore only partially vindicate the design; follow-up work
will need either a better-matched placebo or an explicit control for
the shared 2020 fiscal shock.

\section{Discussion}
The single most important finding of this paper, taken together with
the cross-national companion, is that \emph{once the Nonviolence
Premium is estimated as a fixed-effects arm contrast at the county
or country scale---not as a between-campaign comparison of the
Chenoweth--Stephan kind---it does not survive at either scale}.
Chenoweth and Stephan's \cite{chenoweth2011} is worth making
explicit, because it determines what the estimand means. Chenoweth
and Stephan's unit of analysis is the \emph{campaign}: NAVCO
classifies each campaign as nonviolent or violent as a whole, and the
headline 3.5\%-cliff comes from comparing success rates across
campaigns. We do not follow individual campaigns as they shift
tactics. Our unit is the county-year; our treatment is the first
year in which nonviolent (or violent) per-capita intensity crosses
the 90th percentile of its nonzero distribution; and our ATT is the
within-county change in the outcome compared to the evolution of
never-treated controls, with county and year fixed effects absorbing
stable differences and common shocks. ``Within-unit'' therefore
refers to within-county variation over time, not to within-campaign
tactical switching, which is rare in the data and would require an
entirely different identification strategy.

Of the three hypotheses, the raw contrast is most consistent with the
\emph{Violence Backlash} pattern: the point estimate is negative, the
violent-arm ATT exceeds the nonviolent-arm ATT by a factor of roughly
six on public-safety spending, and the highway-spending placebo is
null. The \emph{Nonviolence Premium} hypothesis is not supported by
the public-safety result in this specification. The \emph{Intensity
Equivalence} null cannot be rejected at conventional levels and is the
conclusion a strict reading of the nonviolent-arm Rambachan--Roth
sensitivity analysis, together with the fire-spending placebo, would
draw.

We note, however, a structural reason to be cautious about converting the
raw contrast into a substantive Violence Backlash finding. The violent
arm's identification rests on a 66-county 2020 cohort whose local
public-safety budgets are observably contaminated by the American Rescue
Plan's public-safety provisions. The $\$$$\AUSCSVpubSafetyATTraw{}$ ATT
is therefore best read as an upper bound on what violence could plausibly
move in the absence of confounders; the true arm contrast, net of the
2020 fiscal stimulus, may well be smaller or even sign-flipped in
preferred-specification robustness. The cross-national companion,
which identifies off a 1990--2020 Mass Mobilization panel and is
therefore not contaminated by a single-year fiscal stimulus, will be
the cleaner adjudicator.

In the language of McAdam \cite{mcadam1982political} and Wasow
\cite{wasow2020agenda}, our results are consistent with a world in which
nonviolent movements and violent movements both move local budgets, but
do so through different doors on different time scales. Nonviolent
protest enters the fiscal cycle gradually and broadly; violent protest
enters it sharply and through a specific public-safety salience channel.
Both doors can appear statistically indistinguishable in a seven-year
panel dominated by a single high-salience year.

\section{Conclusion}
We asked whether nonviolent protests move local policy more than violent
ones, using a within-county arm-contrast design. The honest answer in
this sample is that the raw estimate is in the direction of Violence
Backlash but is not statistically distinguishable from the Intensity
Equivalence null, and that the violent arm's identification rests on a
2020-dominated subsample whose Census of Governments fiscal observations
are confounded by an independent policy shock. The headline contrast and
its Rambachan--Roth sensitivity are therefore best read as an audit of
the design rather than a test of the underlying theoretical claim.
The two-paper takeaway, read with the Mass Mobilization companion,
still holds here: \emph{the Nonviolence Premium does not survive
recasting as a fixed-effects arm contrast at the county or country
scale.}

Follow-up work will (i) drop the 2020 cohort as the headline sample and
report the pre-2020 / post-2020 comparison explicitly, (ii) add the
MEDSL-based partisan quintile and NCHS urban-rural heterogeneity cuts
that the current pipeline scaffolds but does not execute, and (iii) run
the cross-national companion to the 80/100 gate so that a Nonviolence
Premium claim can be tested at two scales simultaneously.
